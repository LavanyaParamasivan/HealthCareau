ECG- Electrocardiogram

An electrocardiogram (ECG) is a test to record the rhythm and electrical activity of your heart.

An electrocardiogram (ECG) is a non-invasive medical test used to measure the electrical activity of the heart.
During an ECG, electrodes are placed on the skin, usually on the chest, arms, and legs.
These electrodes detect the electrical signals generated by the heart as it contracts and pumps blood.
The signals are then recorded and displayed as a graph, showing the heart's rhythm and any abnormalities in
its function. ECGs are commonly used to diagnose various heart conditions, such as arrhythmias, heart attacks,
and abnormalities in the heart's structure. They are an essential tool in cardiology and play a crucial role in
assessing cardiac health and guiding treatment decisions

Types of ECG:

    Resting or standard ECG

  • Conducted while the patient lies down and relaxes
  • Typically takes only a few minutes to complete.
  • Useful for detecting baseline heart activity and identifying irregularities
  • Often performed as part of routine health check-ups or diagnostic evaluations.
  • Provides valuable information on the heart's electrical conduction system.

    Exercise ECG

  • Administered while the patient exercises, usually on a treadmill or exercise bike.
  • Evaluates heart function during physical exertion
  • Helps diagnose conditions like coronary artery disease and assesses cardiovascular fitness.
  • Monitors for abnormal heart rhythms and ischemic changes during exercise.
  • Provides insights into heart health under stress conditions.

    Ambulatory (24-hour) ECG or Holter monitor

  • Involves wearing a portable recorder for up to two weeks
  • Records heart activity over an extended period to capture intermittent abnormalities.
  • Useful for diagnosing arrhythmias that may not occur during a brief ECG
  • Offers continuous monitoring during daily activities and sleep.
  • Enables correlation of symptoms with heart rhythm disturbances for accurate diagnosis.

    ECG procedure

  • Advise the patient to avoid applying oils or moisturizers on the skin before the test.
  • Instruct the patient to wear loose, comfortable clothing that can be easily removed
  • Attach electrodes to specific locations on the chest, arms, and legs using adhesive pads.
  • Ensure proper skin preparation to optimize electrode conductivity and signal acquisition.
  • Start the ECG recording after verifying patient identity and confirming electrode placement.
  • Monitor the ECG waveform for abnormalities such as ST-segment changes, T-wave inversions, and arrhythmias.
